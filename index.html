🔑 Key finding 1 — Most transitions are traceable
54% of transitions were classified as logical and 29% as associative, meaning AI could explain 83% of all human transitions. Only 13% were genuinely opaque. This suggests human word chains are more structured than they appear — but that structure is often cultural or memory-based rather than semantic.
🔑 Key finding 2 — Action words produce the most opaque transitions
Category B (Action) had the highest opaque rate in both Prompt A (8/95) and Prompt B (16/95). Hold had 4 opaque transitions — the most of any single trigger word. Actions seem to unlock more personal, idiosyncratic associations than concrete objects or abstract concepts.
🔑 Key finding 3 — Abstract concepts trigger memory, not logic
Category C (Abstract) had memory as its top connection type (25 instances) — more than any other category. When given abstract words like "Almost," "We," or "Start," the human writer grounded them in concrete personal memories (job anxiety, campus life, San Francisco geography).
🔑 Key finding 4 — Opaque transitions reveal personal identity
The most opaque transitions were not random — they were deeply personal: "Hall → Shane Hollander" (a specific person), "Grace → Mom" (a personal name), "Canvas → School" (an LMS platform), "Month → Asian" (unclear personal anchor). AI could identify these as personal but could not explain them — exactly the limit predicted by Bender et al.
