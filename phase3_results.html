Key observation: Evaluators collectively made 45 "this is human" judgments. Of those, 25 were correct (real human), 15 were Claude, and 5 were GPT. This means Claude accounted for 37.5% of all false positives — nearly as often as real humans were correctly identified.
Phase 2 predictions vs. Phase 3 results
✓ Confirmed
GPT would be easy to identify
Phase 2 predicted: GPT's random drift and aesthetic word repetition would make it obvious. Result: 89% rejection rate. Evaluators almost never called GPT "human" — only 5 times out of 45. The prediction was fully confirmed.
✓ Confirmed
Claude would be harder to distinguish
Phase 2 predicted: Claude's low opaque rate and traceable transitions would cause frequent misidentification. Result: 33% misidentification rate. Claude was called "human" one-third of the time — 3× more than GPT. Confirmed.
~ Partial
Human accuracy would be high
Expected high human identification rate given personal markers. Result: only 56%. Lower than expected — evaluators missed real humans 44% of the time. This suggests human chains were sometimes too idiosyncratic to be recognized as human — personal references may have seemed random.
★ Unexpected
Claude nearly as "human" as real humans
Evaluators identified real humans 56% of the time and Claude 33% of the time. The gap is only 23 percentage points. Claude's compound-word strategy produces chains that feel structured and intentional enough to pass as human over a third of the time — a more humanlike result than anticipated.
Core interpretation
The most striking finding is the three-tier hierarchy of humanness: Human (56%) → Claude (33%) → GPT (11%). This maps almost perfectly onto the Phase 2 connection-type analysis. GPT's 84% opaque rate made it immediately recognizable as non-human. Claude's linguistic compounding strategy — low opaque, traceable transitions — produced chains that feel intentional and structured, mimicking the surface texture of human association without the underlying personal experience.

Critically, human chains were not as recognizable as expected (56%, not ~80%+). This may be because the personal references in human chains (Shane Hollander, Diego, Bay to Breakers) appeared too random to evaluators who lacked that biographical context — the very feature that makes human chains most human (personal specificity) may paradoxically make them harder to identify.

This creates a central tension for the research question: AI can replicate the surface structure of human associative thought well enough to deceive one-third of evaluators, but the mechanism is entirely different — Claude chains from language, humans chain from life.
Limitations
With only 3 participants, statistical conclusions are limited. The results are directionally compelling but not generalizable. For the final paper, frame these as pilot findings that establish patterns worth testing at scale. The consistent ranking across all three participants (Human > Claude > GPT) strengthens the pattern despite the small N.
